Adults Effecting Kids

Colin White

During a discussion earlier today I thought about whether a younger person (13-18) is able to trust an adult (28+?) the same way they would a younger peer. In this instance a woman I know, lets call her Kim, was in a situation that calls for guiding a 14 year old girl, lets call her Ronda, towards a positive future; essentially helping her notice behaviors that act as impediments. However it took two years of Kim being around Ronda before Ronda was willing to even converse with her.

The topic made me wonder whether adults are truly capable of effectively communicating with young people outside of a teaching or mentoring role. I recall myself being much more open to the advice of friends at school than adults whom had much more useful insight. The gap in age and thus the lack of shared experience seems to limit the amount either is truly able to relate with each other and form the bond that allows Kim to truly influence Ronda's decision making.

Other generations have similar gaps although a certain respect seems to bridge the divide allowing for social bonds that our teen years prevent. 

What would it take to build the relationship between an adult and a young person that allows the young person to recognize behaviors that will impede their future success/stability?

Is this merely the result of time? As a student you are with friends for 7-9 hours a day at school (ideally) which allows ample opportunity to form strong bonds through projects, clubs, sports, gossip, etc. As adults there is a cross generational equivalent at work but this generally excludes younger people.

Look for information on the effects of mentoring on kids. If I recall correctly there is some example of success in this regard but it seems to require a significant amount of time. What blocks good advice from being readily accepted and implemented when provided?
